Music as action, not representation

This archive uses the phrase sonic invocation to name sound’s efficacy in Venda Malombo practice. The point is not that music stands for an invisible reality; the point is that it helps produce one. In this frame, rhythm, chant, and repetition are forms of action that reorganize perception, prepare bodies, and facilitate ancestral presence [1].

John Blacking’s writing on Venda possession music is foundational because it refuses to isolate musical structure from lived cultural use. Gilbert Rouget deepens this by treating music and trance as relational rather than mechanically causal, while Victor Turner helps explain how ritual sound opens liminal states in which ordinary social categories become suspended and reconfigured [2].

Music’s power lies in socially embedded structures of meaning rather than abstract universals.
